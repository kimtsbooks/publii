How I Helped My Child Make His Nightmare Less Scary

Kim T. S. May 31, 2022

A gentle way to help children talk about nightmares, process fear, and feel safe again

A few months ago, my son woke up from a nightmare so intense that he couldn’t explain it right away. Like many parents, my first instinct was to reassure him—to remind him that it wasn’t real, that he was safe, and that it was only a dream. But as the days passed and pieces of the dream slowly surfaced, I realized that what he needed most wasn’t for the fear to be dismissed, but for it to be gently understood.

 

One night, as I was tucking him in, he looked at me and asked,

“Do you remember the scary dream I had?”

“The one with the wizard?” I asked.

He nodded. “It was really scary…”

I gently asked, “Do you want to talk about it — and maybe try to make it a little less scary?”

His eyes lit up. “Okay! What if the wizard was like Magic Koopa from Mario?”

We laughed. We made the wizard dance. We made him silly. For a moment, the fear loosened its grip.

Then Kyle grew quiet again.

“There’s more I didn’t tell you, Mom… because it was too scary.”

He told me about an old man trapped inside a helmet. A duck. Pins and needles. And how the duck died.

I could hear how heavy this had been sitting in his little body.

“That is a very sad dream,” I said. “Do you want to try changing it — just a little — so it doesn’t feel so scary anymore?”

He nodded.

So together, we changed the story.

The wizard became a good wizard. The needles became magic needles that stopped the pain. The duck was old and tired, and ready to rest.

The duck’s family was waiting for him. The old man was sad — but he understood. And the duck died peacefully, surrounded by love.

When we finished, Kyle looked at me and said,

“That’s a nice story, Mom.”

“Do you feel better now?” I asked.

“Yes. Thanks, Mommy.”

We hugged for a long time. Just before drifting off to sleep, he lifted his head one last time.

“Mom… did you enjoy talking about my dream and making it nicer?”

“Yes,” I said. “Very much.”

“Me too.”

And then — finally — he fell asleep.
