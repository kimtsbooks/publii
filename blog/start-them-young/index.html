Start Them Young

Kim T. S. December 12, 2021

Do you talk to children about big, complicated topics—things like truth, fairness, leadership, and right versus wrong?

Growing up, those conversations rarely happened in my home. Adults talked; children listened—or were told they were “too young” to understand.

I remember how disempowering that felt. So when Kyle started getting curious about adult conversations, I decided to try something different.

Instead of shutting him out, I began explaining complex ideas in ways he could understand—not to tell him what to think, but to help him learn how to think.

 

Truth vs. Opinion

One evening, we talked about the difference between facts and opinions.

Facts are things we can prove with evidence. Opinions are how we feel about those facts.

Kyle immediately jumped in:

“Like—fun fact: dinosaurs existed!”

Exactly.

But saying someone is cute, funny, or scary? Those are opinions — and different people can feel differently.

Understanding that difference helps kids make sense of the world, especially in a time when they hear so many voices and ideas.

Fairness, Courage, and Doing What’s Right

We also talked about people who stand up for what is right, even when it’s difficult or dangerous.

I explained that some adults choose honesty and courage — telling the truth, protecting others, and standing up to bullies — even when it would be easier to stay quiet.

Kyle listened carefully, then said:

“So… being brave means doing good, even when it’s hard?”

Yes. Exactly.

Respect, Equality, and Kindness

That naturally led to a conversation about respect — being mindful of our words, our actions, and how they affect others.

We talked about being gentle with people who may be smaller, younger, or more vulnerable. About using strength to protect, not to harm.

And about kindness — especially when we’re feeling silly, angry, or frustrated.

He thought about it quietly, then promised:

“I’ll try to be kind and respectful.”

That’s all I can really hope for—not perfection, but awareness.

Children don’t need to understand everything right away. But they can understand values. They can learn empathy. And they can learn how to ask questions and think critically.

Our children are growing up in a complex world. Starting these conversations early helps them navigate it with confidence and compassion.

Believe there is good in the world. Be the good.
